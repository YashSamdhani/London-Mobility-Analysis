🗺 What is this study about?

This is a human mobility analysis derived from mobile phone location data. Researchers detected where people stopped (stay-points), inferred what they were doing there (activity inference), then measured how people move between locations (origin-destination flows). The goal is to understand patterns of movement — useful for transport planning, epidemiology, urban design, and privacy risk assessment.

📊 The 11 charts form a narrative arc

⚠️ Key finding in one sentence

London residents show highly predictable, local mobility (median range 17 km, strong self-loops in the O-D matrix), but flow patterns follow a power law, meaning a tiny fraction of corridors handle a disproportionate share of all trips — and individual movement signatures are distinctive enough to pose re-identification privacy risks.

01

Activity Patterns

What Londoners are doing, when, and in what sequence

What is a "stay point"?

A stay point is a location where someone paused for a meaningful amount of time (e.g. >10 minutes). A person moving through the city generates a chain of GPS pings; algorithms cluster stationary pings into stay-points, then each is labelled with an activity type based on time-of-day, land use, and frequency.

Stay Count by Activity

Median Stay Duration

Home vs Work duration gap

Home stays last ~480 min (overnight/evening anchors). Work ~150 min. Shopping/eating stays are short bursts of 15–25 min. "Others" catch-all has short median — many brief transits.

Why are "Others" and "Home" the top two categories?

"Others" (216,766 stays) includes all unclassifiable stops — transit hubs, car parks, ambiguous residential/commercial areas. Home (170,151) dominates because people spend most time at their residence, especially overnight when GPS captures long stationary periods. The remaining 6 categories share the rest.

Activity Transition Matrix — how often activity A is followed by activity B

Dominant transitions: Others ↔ Others (93,470) and Home → Others (66,262)

The largest flows are Others→Others (chaining unclassified stops) and Home→Others — leaving home to do something unspecified. Work→Work self-loops (13,156) indicate multi-session work stays in the same day. Education rarely chains to entertainment — students go home first.

📅 Weekly pattern

Monday and Tuesday show the highest stay volumes (~95K each). Wednesday–Thursday dip slightly (~82K). Friday recovers. Saturday and Sunday are clearly lower — about 60–70K stays — reflecting reduced commuting. This strongly validates the dataset's temporal realism.

Origin–Destination Flows

Where people travel between zones, and how those flows are distributed

What is an O-D matrix?

An Origin-Destination (O-D) matrix is a table where rows are departure zones and columns are arrival zones. Each cell holds the number of trips between that pair. With ~7,000 MSOA zones, the full matrix has ~49 million cells — most are zero (people rarely travel between distant zones). The heatmap shows only the top 50 origin zones for clarity.

Why log scale?

Flows span several orders of magnitude. Without log scale, 99% of cells would appear black. Log scale compresses the range so both large corridors (10,000+ trips) and small ones (2–10 trips) are visible simultaneously.

Zipf / Power law in mobility

The Rank-Size chart plots corridors from most-used (rank 1, ~50,000 trips) to least-used (rank ~1.5M, 2 trips). The power-law fit with α = 1.43 means this follows Zipf-like scaling — the 2nd-ranked corridor has about half the flow of the 1st, the 10th has ~1/10th, etc. This is universal in urban mobility data.

Work vs. All-activity divergence

The scatter plot shows many corridors with high all-activity flow but near-zero work flow. These are leisure/residential corridors. Conversely, a few corridors are almost exclusively work trips (work fraction ≈ 1.0) — likely suburb-to-business-district flows.

O-D Uniqueness Metrics — what each one measures

Shannon Entropy (Work) — Median 4.929 bits
Measures how spread out a zone's outgoing work trips are. High entropy = trips go to many destinations roughly equally. Low entropy = most trips go to 1–2 places. The near-bell distribution centered at ~5 bits means most zones send workers to diverse destinations.
Entropy All — Median 5.000 bits
Same as above but for all activities. Slightly wider distribution (goes to ~8 bits) because leisure trips are more diffuse than work trips. The higher maximum reflects zones with extremely diverse non-work travel.
Gini Coefficient (Work) — Median 0.389
Measures inequality of flow distribution. 0 = perfectly equal flows to all destinations. 1 = all flow to one destination. Median 0.39 means mild concentration — zones have a few preferred destinations but aren't monopolised by one.
HHI Work — Median 0.051
Herfindahl-Hirschman Index: sum of squared market shares. Near-zero median (0.051) confirms flows are spread across many destinations. Very right-skewed: a few zones have HHI > 0.5, meaning one destination absorbs >70% of work trips.
Location Quotient (LQ Work) — Median 0.980
Compares a zone's work flow to what you'd expect if trips were distributed proportionally to zone size. LQ ≈ 1 = average. LQ > 1 = over-represented work destination. Long right tail shows some zones attract far more workers than their size would predict (employment centres).
Asymmetry — Median 0.182
How different inbound flows are from outbound flows. 0 = symmetric (as many people come as leave). High values = strong directional bias. Median 0.18 is low, but a long tail means some zones are strongly directional — e.g. a residential zone that exports workers but imports few.

Entropy–Gini scatter: what the cloud shape tells us

High entropy + low Gini (top-right) = diverse but balanced — trips scatter widely with no dominant corridor. Low entropy + low Gini (top-left) = balanced local flows — few destinations but equally used. Low entropy + high Gini (bottom-left) = dominant single destination — most trips go to one place. The vast majority of zones cluster in the medium-high entropy, medium Gini range — typical urban zone with 5–20 significant destinations.

What is Radius of Gyration (r_g)?

Borrowed from physics, the radius of gyration characterises the spatial spread of an individual's movement. It is the root-mean-square distance of all their visited locations from their centre of mass. A person who only commutes 3 km to work has a low r_g; a travelling salesperson covering the UK has a high r_g. It is a single-number summary of how wide a person's "mobility territory" is over a period.

The bimodal bump at 5–20 km

The histogram shows two peaks near 5 km and 15–18 km. This likely reflects two subpopulations: hyper-local users (walking/cycling distance) and typical commuters using underground/rail. November 2021 was during post-COVID return to work, so commute distances may be slightly compressed.

Privacy implications

A small radius of gyration makes re-identification easier — if someone only visits 3–4 locations, those unique patterns can be matched against public data (home + workplace + gym). Paradoxically, the very people with predictable, small-range movement are the most privacy-vulnerable in mobility datasets.

Temporal activity profiles — the double-peak workday

The hourly distribution shows a morning surge at 8–9 AM (commute start) and a larger afternoon peak at 14–16 PM (possibly school pickups + early work departures) before dropping off through evening. The stacked-area chart shows Work activity dominates 8–18h, while Home/Others dominate the overnight period. Entertainment stays are a thin evening layer visible 18–22h.

04

Sensitive Points of Interest

Why location data near hospitals, schools, and worship sites raises privacy concerns

Why are these POIs "sensitive"?

Visiting certain types of locations can reveal private information about a person: a hospital visit may imply illness, an addiction clinic reveals substance issues, a place of worship reveals religion, a school presence could indicate a child, a military base may have national security implications. Mobility data that links individuals to these places must be handled with extra care under GDPR and similar regulations.

Sensitive POI Count — Greater London Area

Pharmacies are the most numerous (1,016)

London has more pharmacies than schools (904). Their density means almost any mobility trace passes within a few hundred metres of one — making proximity-based inference highly feasible even without direct entry data.

Places of worship (590) and spatial clustering

Faith buildings are concentrated in specific communities. Detecting repeated visits to a mosque, synagogue, or church can infer religion with high confidence — a protected characteristic under UK and EU equality law.

Military sites (79)

79 military installations across the GLA is surprising — reflecting barracks, TA centres, MoD offices, and former military land. Mobility data from personnel at these sites is a national security concern.

Spatial pattern: denser centre, spread suburbs

The scatter map shows POIs concentrate in inner London (around 51.5°N, -0.1°W) but spread across the entire GLA footprint. No area of London is free of sensitive POIs, meaning any sufficiently precise mobility dataset will contain sensitive proximity events.

Mobility Network Communities

How zones cluster based on work-trip flows using graph community detection

What is community detection on a mobility network?

Zones are treated as nodes in a graph; work-trip flows between zones become weighted edges. The Louvain algorithm then finds groups of zones that have strong internal flows relative to their external flows — these are "functional regions" where people predominantly work within the community rather than crossing into others.

Why relatively even sizes?

The communities range from 330 to 1,050 zones — a 3:1 ratio, which is fairly balanced for a 7,000-zone city. This suggests London's labour market is polycentric: rather than one dominant centre (City of London) pulling all commute flows, there are roughly 10 functional sub-regions. This aligns with London's orbital rail and bus structure.

Policy application

These communities could inform transport authority catchment areas, school admission zones, or health region boundaries. If communities cross borough boundaries (which they often do), it highlights misalignment between administrative and functional geography.

GL

Glossary of Terms

Plain-language definitions for every technical concept used in this analysis

MSOA — Middle Super Output Area
A UK census geography unit containing ~8,000 residents. Greater London has ~983 MSOAs. They are the spatial building blocks for this analysis — each zone is an MSOA.
Stay-Point
A location where a mobile device was stationary for a minimum threshold time (e.g. 10 min). Detected from GPS/CDR traces by clustering stationary pings. Represents a place-visit event.
Origin-Destination (O-D) Matrix
A matrix where entry [i,j] counts trips from zone i to zone j. Diagonal entries are intra-zone trips. Rows sum to total outbound trips from each zone.
Radius of Gyration (r_g)
Root-mean-square distance of all visited locations from the centroid of movement. Summarises how widely an individual roams. Units: kilometres.
Shannon Entropy
From information theory: measures how uncertain (or diverse) a probability distribution is. Here, applied to the distribution of trips from one zone across all destination zones. Max = log₂(N) bits. High = diverse destinations.
Gini Coefficient
Measures inequality: 0 = perfectly equal (all destinations get equal trips), 1 = monopoly (all trips go to one destination). Borrowed from economics (wealth inequality). Here applied to destination concentration.
HHI — Herfindahl-Hirschman Index
Sum of squared market shares of destinations. Ranges 0–1. HHI < 0.1 = low concentration. HHI > 0.25 = high concentration. More sensitive than Gini to dominant players.
Location Quotient (LQ)
LQ = (zone's share of flows) / (expected share if uniformly distributed). LQ > 1 = over-represented destination. LQ < 1 = under-represented. Identifies functional specialisation.
Power Law / Zipf Distribution
A distribution where the frequency of an item is inversely proportional to its rank raised to exponent α. Common in city sizes, word frequencies, and mobility flows. α = 1.43 here is shallower than pure Zipf (α = 1), meaning top corridors are less dominant than in some cities.
Louvain Algorithm
A graph-partitioning algorithm that maximises "modularity" — the degree to which a network has more internal connections within communities than expected by chance. Fast, widely used for community detection in large networks.
Modularity (Q)
Quality metric for community detection. Ranges −1 to 1. Higher Q means better-defined communities. Values above 0.3 are generally considered meaningful structure.
Activity Transition Matrix
A square matrix where entry [A,B] counts how often activity A was immediately followed by activity B in the same person's daily sequence. Diagonal = repeated same-activity stays.
Sensitive POI
A Point of Interest whose visitation could reveal protected personal information (health status, religion, legal status). Under GDPR Article 9, inferred data about these characteristics has heightened protection requirements.
Spearman ρ (rho)
Rank correlation coefficient between two variables. Ranges −1 to 1. ρ = 0.611 between work and all-activity flows means they are moderately positively correlated — corridors busy with work tend to be busy overall, but the relationship is imperfect.
CDR — Call Detail Records
Logs generated by mobile networks recording when/where a phone connected (calls, texts, data). Coarser than GPS (cell-tower level), but available for billions of devices and widely used in population-level mobility research.
Asymmetry Index
Measures directional imbalance of flows: |outflow − inflow| / (outflow + inflow). 0 = balanced. 1 = all one-directional. High asymmetry zones are either net exporters (residential) or net importers (employment hubs) of people.
